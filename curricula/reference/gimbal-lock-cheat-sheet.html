Gimbal lock and quaternions

What becomes singular, what stays valid, and what PX4 still has to handle

What locks?

In a 3-2-1 yaw–pitch–roll sequence, pitch at ±90° makes the first yaw axis and third roll axis collinear. Three angle controls then describe only two independent instantaneous rotation directions.

The observable symptom

pitch → ±90°
yaw and roll → coupled / non-unique

The orientation remains real and valid; its Euler coordinates become ambiguous. Angle rates may become very large near the singularity.

Why quaternions avoid it

q = [w,x,y,z]
w²+x²+y²+z² = 1

A quaternion stores one axis-and-angle rotation, not three sequential rotations. There is no middle Euler angle whose axes can collapse.

The tradeoff

Quaternions use four constrained numbers for three rotational degrees of freedom. They also double-cover orientation: q and −q are the same physical attitude.

What quaternions do not fix

ProblemQuaternion helps?Reason
Euler-coordinate singularityYesAttitude state no longer depends on a three-angle chart.
Mechanical axes becoming collinearNoSoftware representation cannot create a missing actuator direction.
Joint travel limits or cable wrapNoThese are physical constraints.
Euler display jumping near ±90° pitchNot necessarilyConverting a valid quaternion back to Euler angles reintroduces the singular chart.

PX4 reading rule

quaternion state: globally nonsingular orientation
Euler readout: convenient local coordinates
gimbal joints: real mechanical constraints

Do not diagnose a quaternion failure from an Euler-angle jump alone. Compare the quaternion or rotate a reference vector before concluding that the physical attitude jumped.
