Lesson 0001 · PX4 orientation math

Frames before quaternions

Tangible win: after this lesson you can predict the gimbal joint command from a vehicle heading and an earth target—and explain exactly why the Gazebo bug adds vehicle heading.

You do not need differential equations for this lesson. The first layer is compass geometry and subtraction. Quaternions arrive later because the real vehicle can roll and pitch as well as yaw.

1. One angle, two possible reference points

Earth frame · NED

Yaw zero points north. East is +90°. The reference does not rotate with the vehicle.

Vehicle frame · FRD

Yaw zero points through the vehicle nose. The reference rotates whenever the vehicle rotates.

The number 0° is therefore incomplete by itself. Earth-frame 0° means “north.” Vehicle-frame 0° means “straight through the nose.”

3. Derive the subtraction

  1. Gazebo adds the joint rotation to the vehicle rotation.
    actual earth yaw = vehicle earth yaw + vehicle-relative joint yaw
  2. We want the actual yaw to equal the earth target. Undo the vehicle rotation by moving it to the other side.
    vehicle-relative joint yaw = earth target yaw − vehicle earth yaw
  3. For east vehicle and north target:
    joint = 0° − 90° = −90°
    actual = 90° + (−90°) = 0°

The broken bridge skips step 2. It sends the earth target directly to the body-relative joint. For the issue case that means joint 0°, so the gimbal stays forward and ends up east.

5. Practice before revealing the next layer

6. Quaternion preview

Open only after the yaw subtraction feels natural

Yaw subtraction works because all rotations in this simplified picture use one axis. A flying vehicle also rolls and pitches. Three-dimensional rotations do not generally commute, so PX4 stores the complete rotation as a quaternion.

q_joint = inverse(q_vehicle) × q_target

This is the same “undo the vehicle, then express the target” idea. Quaternion inverse replaces the minus sign, and quaternion multiplication replaces angle addition. The PR implements this as:

vehicle_attitude.inversed() * attitude_setpoint

Lesson 0003 explains why a yaw quaternion uses the half-angle and why multiplication order matters. You do not need that algebra to understand the bug signature.

7. PX4 source exercise

Goal: prove the frame meaning from source, then predict the simulator result before using a listener.

  1. Open VehicleAttitude.msg. Record the quaternion order and the exact source and destination frames of q.
  2. Open GimbalDeviceSetAttitude.msg. Locate the vehicle-frame and earth-frame yaw flags. Explain why flags 32 and 64 cannot be interpreted identically.
  3. Open GZGimbal.cpp. Find where the incoming quaternion is converted to Euler angles. Before running SITL, predict the actual earth yaw for vehicle yaw +90°, earth target 0°, when no earth-to-vehicle conversion occurs.
  4. Verify the prediction with listener vehicle_attitude, listener gimbal_device_set_attitude, and listener gimbal_device_attitude_status. Record the three yaws, the flags, and whether the vehicle yaw leaked into the gimbal result.

Evidence to keep: source location, prediction, listener values, and one-sentence conclusion.
