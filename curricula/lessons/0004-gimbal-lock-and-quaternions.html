Lesson 0004 · PX4 orientation math

Gimbal lock is a failure of coordinates, not orientation

Tangible win: after this lesson you can identify the exact moment yaw and roll stop being independent—and explain why a quaternion remains valid there.

The phrase gimbal lock comes from nested mechanical gimbals, but the same geometry appears when we describe attitude with three sequential Euler angles.

1. Three rotations are not always three independent axes

PX4 commonly displays attitude as roll, pitch, and yaw. Think of these as three ordered rotation instructions, not three permanent earth axes:

Yaw → pitch → roll

Each instruction changes the axes available to the instructions that follow it.

The dangerous middle angle

For the 3-2-1 sequence, pitch at ±90° makes the yaw and roll axes collinear.

When two axes lie on the same line, turning one or the other produces the same kind of motion. You have three angle labels but only two independent rotation directions.

2. Move the mechanism into lock

Move pitch toward 90°. The left picture shows the yaw axis and the vehicle's roll axis. The right picture compares two different Euler commands.

Yaw–roll axis separation30°
A–B orientation difference10.3°

The axes are still independent, but they are converging.

At exactly 90°, both commands produce the same attitude. Their yaw and roll values are different, but only the combination roll − yaw remains observable:

A: roll − yaw = 0° − 20° = −20°
B: roll − yaw = −20° − 0° = −20°

This is the singularity: the orientation is fine, but the map from orientation to one unique roll–pitch–yaw triple has failed.

3. Why the math becomes unstable nearby

Near 90°, the axes are almost—rather than exactly—the same. A controller trying to request a small physical rotation may need very large, opposing yaw and roll rates. In the Euler-rate equations this appears as division by cos(pitch):

pitch → 90°  ⇒  cos(pitch) → 0  ⇒  Euler rates can blow up

This does not mean the vehicle suddenly rotated infinitely fast. It means these particular coordinates are becoming a poor way to describe an ordinary motion.

4. A quaternion never chooses three sequential axes

A unit quaternion stores one axis and one angle as four constrained numbers. There is no middle Euler angle that can rotate two of its coordinate axes onto each other:

q = [ cos(θ/2), axis · sin(θ/2) ]
w² + x² + y² + z² = 1

The extra number is not an extra physical degree of freedom. The unit-length constraint removes it. That redundancy lets quaternions cover every 3D orientation without an Euler-style singular point.

5. Cross 90° without stopping

Scrub a smooth physical pitch through 90°. The quaternion changes continuously. A conventional Euler readout must become ambiguous and then switch to a different—but equivalent—set of angle labels.

Euler singularity

Quaternion state — continuous

[0.707, 0, 0.707, 0]

Euler labels — ambiguous here

roll = any matching value
pitch = 90°
yaw = coupled to roll

The orientation is valid. Only the Euler naming is non-unique.

6. What quaternions do—and do not—save

They do save the attitude representation

PX4 can estimate, integrate, compare, and compose orientation without encountering an Euler coordinate singularity.

They do not save a locked mechanism

If two physical gimbal axes align, software cannot invent the missing actuator direction. Joint limits and cable wrap also remain real.

Converting a healthy quaternion back to Euler angles can also reintroduce the jump. That is why an odd roll–pitch–yaw display near ±90° is not, by itself, evidence that the quaternion or physical attitude jumped.

7. Apply this distinction to PX4

PX4 stores vehicle attitude as a Hamilton quaternion that rotates body FRD into earth NED. That state remains usable at every vehicle pitch. Modules may still convert it to Euler angles for displays or real joint commands, where sequence conventions and physical constraints matter again.

quaternion state → nonsingular orientation math
Euler readout → convenient local labels
gimbal joints → actual mechanical constraints

8. Retrieve the core idea

At 90° pitch, the vehicle still has a valid orientation. What actually failed?

One careful wording

Should I say “quaternions prevent gimbal lock”?

For software attitude representation, that shorthand is common: quaternions avoid the Euler-angle singularity called gimbal lock. More precisely, they do not prevent physical gimbal axes from aligning; they prevent the orientation state from being expressed through a singular three-angle coordinate chart.

9. PX4 source exercise

Goal: observe where PX4's quaternion remains smooth while an Euler conversion reaches its singular coordinate chart.

  1. Open Euler.hpp and locate the constructor from a quaternion. Follow its conversion through Dcm.hpp.
  2. Open Quaternion.hpp. Record why the underlying quaternion operations do not require a yaw–pitch–roll sequence.
  3. Using PX4 matrix types, generate pure-pitch quaternions from 80° through 100°. Convert each to Eulerf and also rotate the body-forward vector.
  4. Before running the test, predict which output may jump and which physical vector should move continuously. Verify, then explain why an Euler jump is not sufficient evidence of a vehicle-attitude jump.

Evidence to keep: the tested angle table, Euler outputs, rotated-vector outputs, and the source branches responsible for the reported Euler representation.
