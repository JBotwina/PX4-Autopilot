Lesson 0003 · PX4 orientation math

Quaternions are rotations you can compose

Tangible win: after this lesson you can read a yaw-only quaternion, reverse it with an inverse, and follow the PX4 conversion q_vehicle⁻¹ × q_target.

A quaternion is not a mysterious four-dimensional heading. For this lesson, treat it as a carefully designed container for two things: a rotation axis and an amount of rotation.

1. Learn the four slots

w — scalar part

Stores the cosine of half the rotation angle.

[x,y,z] — vector part

Points along the rotation axis, scaled by the sine of half the angle.

q = [ cos(θ/2), axisₓ·sin(θ/2), axisᵧ·sin(θ/2), axis_z·sin(θ/2) ]

PX4 uses Hamilton quaternions in scalar-first order: [w,x,y,z]. A pure yaw turns around the Z/down axis, so x=0 and y=0:

q_yaw(ψ) = [ cos(ψ/2), 0, 0, sin(ψ/2) ]

2. Watch physical angle become quaternion angle

The left circle shows the vehicle. The right circle plots w horizontally and z vertically. Move the yaw and notice that the quaternion point travels through only half the angle.

3. Why use a half-angle?

PX4 rotates a vector by placing the vector between the quaternion and its inverse:

v_rotated = q × [0,v] × q⁻¹

The quaternion participates twice—once on each side. Encoding half the desired angle makes the complete sandwich produce the full physical rotation. You do not need to expand that multiplication yet; remember the operational rule: physical angle goes in, half-angle appears inside q.

4. The inverse means “undo this rotation”

For a unit quaternion, inversion is pleasantly simple:

q⁻¹ = [w, −x, −y, −z]

Toggle Show inverse above. On the quaternion circle it reflects across the w axis. On the physical compass it reverses the rotation. Applying a rotation and then its inverse returns the identity:

q × q⁻¹ = [1,0,0,0]

5. Multiplication means “compose rotations”

PX4's Hamilton convention uses this order:

q_result = q₂ × q₁   means   first q₁, then q₂

For yaw-only rotations, composition behaves like angle addition. In full 3D, multiplication order matters: roll-then-yaw is generally different from yaw-then-roll.

6. Apply it to the gimbal PR

The vehicle quaternion maps the vehicle frame into earth. The target quaternion also ends in earth. To express the target relative to the vehicle, first undo the vehicle rotation:

q_joint = q_vehicle⁻¹ × q_target

7. Retrieve it

A pure +90° yaw has x=0 and y=0. Predict w and z to three decimals.

8. One strange but useful fact

Why do 0° and 360° show different quaternion numbers?

The 0° quaternion is [1,0,0,0]. The 360° quaternion is [−1,0,0,0]. These are negatives of each other, but they represent the same physical orientation. In general, q and −q describe the same rotation. This is called the quaternion double cover.
